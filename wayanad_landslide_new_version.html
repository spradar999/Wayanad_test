A landslide is the rapid downward movement of rock, soil, and debris along a slope due to gravity. It typically occurs when the stability of a slope is compromised by natural or human-induced factors. Heavy rainfall, earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, deforestation, and construction activities can trigger landslides by weakening the materials that support a slope. There are various types of landslides, such as rockfalls, debris flows, and slumps, depending on the material involved and the movement pattern. Landslides can cause significant damage to infrastructure, disrupt transportation, destroy vegetation, and pose threats to human lives. Mountainous and hilly regions with loose or saturated soil are particularly vulnerable. Monitoring slope stability, managing land use, and implementing early warning systems are key to reducing landslide risks. With climate change increasing the frequency of extreme weather events, landslides are becoming more common and dangerous, highlighting the need for effective mitigation and preparedness strategies.
